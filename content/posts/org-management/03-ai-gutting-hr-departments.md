## IBM Just Showed Everyone the Endgame

In May 2025, IBM made an announcement that should have been front-page news in every business publication. Their internal AI agent, [AskHR, now handles 94% of routine HR questions](https://www.ibm.com/case-studies/ibm-askhr) across the entire company. Not 94% attempted — 94% resolved without human intervention. The system generated over 11.5 million employee interactions in 2024 alone and reduced support tickets by 75% since its initial deployment.

The immediate consequence: IBM's CEO confirmed plans to [shift HR headcount budget toward sales and engineering](https://www.entrepreneur.com/business-news/ibm-ceo-ai-replaced-hundreds-of-human-resources-staff/491341). The HR Business Partner role — the generalist who sits between employees and the HR function — has been effectively eliminated except for senior leadership support.

This isn't a pilot program at a scrappy startup. This is a 280,000-person enterprise that spent nearly a decade iterating on AI-powered HR, starting with CogniPay in 2018 for AI-driven compensation recommendations and expanding outward from there. IBM's CHRO Nickle Lamoureux told analyst Josh Bersin that the AI agent now writes performance reviews, creates development plans, and coaches managers through complex performance decisions.

Bersin's response was blunt: he expects [AI to drive a 20-30% reduction in HR headcount per employee](https://joshbersin.com/2025/05/yes-hr-organizations-will-partially-be-replaced-by-ai-and-thats-good/) across the industry. Not at some distant future date. Now. Within current budget cycles.

If you work in HR, manage an HR team, or depend on HR services as a line manager, the ground beneath you shifted last year. This post maps the new terrain.

## The Four HR Functions Getting Automated First

Not all of HR is equally exposed. The functions getting replaced fastest share common characteristics: they're high-volume, rule-based, and historically dependent on humans acting as intermediaries between employees and information systems.

### 1. Employee Service Desk and Benefits Administration

This is where IBM started, and it's the lowest-hanging fruit for every company. The majority of HR interactions are transactional: How do I enroll in dental insurance? What's the PTO policy for bereavement? How do I update my direct deposit?

These questions follow predictable patterns. An AI agent trained on company policy documents can answer them with higher accuracy and lower latency than a human generalist who has to look up the same documents. IBM's 94% containment rate proves the concept at scale.

The tools enabling this aren't exotic. [ServiceNow's HR Service Delivery](https://www.servicenow.com/products/hr-service-delivery.html) platform, Workday's assistant features, and a growing crop of startups like Leena AI and Espressive are all competing in this space. Most enterprise HR tech vendors now ship some version of an AI-powered service desk.

The displacement is real. Companies running these tools typically reduce their HR service center headcount by 40-60% within the first two years. The remaining staff handles escalations, edge cases, and the kind of emotionally complex situations (harassment complaints, accommodation requests, termination logistics) where AI still falls short.

### 2. Resume Screening and Initial Candidate Filtering

The volume problem in recruiting is staggering. A single job posting on LinkedIn can generate hundreds or thousands of applications. Before AI tools, recruiters spent roughly [23 hours screening resumes](https://www.apollotechnical.com/hr-statistics/) for a single hire. Most of that time was spent on candidates who were clearly unqualified.

AI screening tools from vendors like HireVue, Pymetrics (now part of Harver), and Eightfold AI now handle initial filtering at scale. They parse resumes, match skills to job requirements, and produce ranked candidate lists. Some incorporate video interview analysis, scoring candidates on communication patterns and competency signals.

The efficiency gain is undeniable. But here's where the story gets complicated — and where the courtroom enters the picture.

### 3. Compensation Analysis and Pay Equity

IBM's CogniPay was ahead of its time in 2018, using AI to generate pay recommendations based on skill assessments rather than job titles alone. The broader market has caught up. Tools like Syndio, Payscale, and Carta Total Comp now offer AI-driven compensation benchmarking that can flag pay equity issues, recommend adjustments, and model the cost impact of proposed changes in real time.

The old model — where a comp analyst spent weeks building spreadsheets from salary survey data — is being compressed into minutes. More importantly, AI tools can continuously monitor for pay gaps across gender, race, and other protected categories, something that annual audits fundamentally couldn't do.

This is one area where AI in HR genuinely improves outcomes rather than just cutting costs. Pay equity lawsuits cost companies [an average of $2.5 million to settle](https://www.shrm.org/topics-tools/news/employee-relations/average-cost-of-employment-lawsuits), and continuous monitoring is cheaper than periodic audits that discover problems after they've compounded for years.

### 4. Learning and Development Recommendations

The corporate training industry has been ripe for disruption for decades. Most L&D programs operate on the "catalog" model — here's a list of courses, pick what interests you. Completion rates are abysmal, relevance is questionable, and the connection between training and actual performance improvement is tenuous at best.

AI-powered platforms like Degreed, EdCast (now part of Cornerstone), and LinkedIn Learning's recommendation engine are shifting toward personalized development paths. They analyze an employee's current skills, their role requirements, their career trajectory, and the company's strategic priorities, then recommend specific learning sequences.

The more sophisticated implementations — like what WPP built using OpenAI and [Reejig](https://reejig.com/) — go further. WPP used AI to rationalize 65,000 job titles down to 600 and then mapped skills gaps across the entire organization, giving L&D teams actual data about where to invest training budgets rather than relying on manager gut feelings.

## The Bias Problem Nobody Solved

Here's where the optimistic narrative about AI in HR runs into a wall. The same tools that screen resumes faster and more consistently than humans also encode and amplify bias in ways that are proving genuinely dangerous.

### The Workday Lawsuit

In one of the most significant AI employment cases working through U.S. courts, Derek Mobley filed suit against [Workday alleging that its AI-powered screening tools discriminated against him](https://fairnow.ai/workday-lawsuit-resume-screening/) based on race, age, and disability. Mobley, who is Black and over 40, applied to more than 100 jobs through employers using Workday's platform and was rejected by all of them.

The case reached a critical milestone in May 2025 when the court [granted conditional certification as a collective action under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA)](https://www.lawandtheworkplace.com/2025/06/ai-bias-lawsuit-against-workday-reaches-next-stage-as-court-grants-conditional-certification-of-adea-claim/). The class now includes all applicants aged 40 and over who applied through Workday's platform from September 2020 onward and were denied.

This matters for two reasons. First, it establishes that AI vendors — not just the employers using their tools — can be held liable for discriminatory outcomes. Second, the collective action certification means the financial exposure for Workday could be enormous.

Workday has denied all claims and insists its tools don't make hiring decisions. But the court's willingness to let the case proceed signals that "the algorithm did it" isn't going to work as a defense.

### The ACLU vs. Aon

In a separate action, the [ACLU challenged three of Aon's AI hiring assessment tools](https://www.fisherphillips.com/en/news-insights/another-employer-faces-ai-hiring-bias-lawsuit.html) — ADEPT-15, vidAssess-AI, and gridChallenge — alleging they discriminate against people with disabilities and certain racial groups. The complaint also targeted Aon's marketing, which promoted the tools as "bias-free."

The vidAssess-AI tool is particularly controversial. It analyzes video interviews for behavioral signals, a methodology that disability rights advocates argue inherently disadvantages candidates with conditions affecting speech patterns, facial expressions, or motor control.

### Research Confirms the Pattern

The lawsuits aren't outliers. Research [published through VoxDev in May 2025](https://sanfordheisler.com/blog/2025/12/ai-bias-in-hiring-algorithmic-recruiting-and-your-rights/) found that AI hiring tools systematically favored female applicants over Black male applicants with identical qualifications. The bias wasn't random — it was consistent and directional, suggesting the training data itself encoded historical discrimination patterns.

This creates a genuine dilemma for HR leaders. The efficiency gains from AI screening are real and significant. But deploying tools that produce discriminatory outcomes exposes the company to litigation, reputational damage, and regulatory action — especially as states like Illinois, New York City, and Colorado have enacted or proposed AI hiring transparency laws.

## The Entry-Level Pipeline Crisis

Korn Ferry's [2026 Talent Acquisition Trends report](https://www.kornferry.com/insights/featured-topics/talent-recruitment/talent-acquisition-trends) identified what might be the most consequential second-order effect of AI in HR: the gutting of entry-level positions.

The numbers are stark. [43% of companies plan to replace roles with AI](https://www.kornferry.com/insights/featured-topics/talent-recruitment/talent-acquisition-trends), with operations/back-office staff (58%) and entry-level positions (37%) as the top targets.

The boardroom logic is straightforward. Entry-level employees are expensive relative to their immediate output. They require training, supervision, and time to become productive. An AI agent that handles the same tasks costs a fraction of the salary with no onboarding period.

But this logic ignores where future leaders come from. Most managers and directors didn't parachute into their roles. They started by filing papers, running reports, coordinating meetings, and doing the routine work that taught them how the business actually operates. Cut those entry-level roles, and in three to five years you face a leadership pipeline that's empty.

Korn Ferry puts it directly: "Your cost saving today is rapidly becoming your talent crisis tomorrow."

The companies getting this right are those that redesign entry-level roles rather than eliminate them. Instead of hiring a coordinator to schedule meetings and compile reports (tasks AI handles trivially), they hire an analyst who works alongside AI tools, learns to evaluate their output, and develops the judgment that will make them an effective manager in five years.

## What "AI-First HR" Actually Looks Like

If the old model was a large HR department processing transactions and answering questions, the new model is a smaller team doing fundamentally different work. Here's what the emerging structure looks like at companies that have made the transition thoughtfully.

### The Shrinking Core

A mid-size company (1,000-5,000 employees) that previously maintained an HR team of 30-50 people is likely to stabilize at 15-25 within the next two years if they deploy AI tools aggressively. The reductions come almost entirely from:

- **Service desk and benefits administration** (replaced by AI agents)
- **Recruiting coordination** (replaced by ATS automation and AI screening)
- **Comp analysis and reporting** (replaced by continuous monitoring tools)
- **Basic L&D administration** (replaced by personalized learning platforms)

### The Roles That Grow

While headcount shrinks, certain roles become more important and more senior:

**AI Operations Manager (HR):** Someone needs to train, monitor, and audit the AI tools. This role didn't exist three years ago. It requires a combination of HR domain knowledge and technical literacy — understanding what the models are doing well enough to catch when they're producing biased or incorrect outputs.

**Employee Experience Designer:** With transactions automated, the remaining human touchpoints become disproportionately important. The onboarding experience, the manager coaching session, the career development conversation — these moments now carry more weight because they're among the few times an employee interacts with an actual human in HR.

**Change Management Specialist:** IBM didn't automate its HR function overnight. It took nearly a decade of iterating, training, and managing employee expectations. Every company going through this transition needs people who can manage the human side of the change — and ironically, this is one of the things AI still does poorly.

**Org Design Strategist:** With AI tools providing real-time data on skills gaps, performance patterns, and workforce composition, companies can redesign their organizations more frequently and with better data. The strategist who can interpret this data and translate it into structural decisions becomes essential.

### The Critical Thinking Gap

Korn Ferry's survey revealed a striking disconnect. While executives and boards rank AI skills as the most-wanted capability for new hires, [73% of talent acquisition leaders rank critical thinking as their number-one recruiting priority](https://www.kornferry.com/insights/featured-topics/talent-recruitment/talent-acquisition-trends). AI skills ranked fifth.

The TA leaders are right. You can teach someone to use ChatGPT in a week. Teaching them to evaluate whether its output makes sense, spot the errors, and know when human judgment should override machine logic — that takes years of practice and a fundamentally different cognitive skill set.

The companies hiring for AI proficiency are solving yesterday's problem. The tools will keep getting easier to use. The companies hiring for critical thinking are building teams that can adapt to whatever the tools become next.

## The Hybrid Workforce Nobody Planned For

Korn Ferry's most provocative finding: more than half of talent leaders are planning to add autonomous AI agents to their teams in 2026 — not as tools, but as something closer to digital teammates with their own identities, permissions, and responsibilities.

This raises questions that HR frameworks aren't remotely prepared to answer:

- **If an AI agent makes a hiring recommendation that produces discriminatory outcomes, who is accountable?** The vendor? The company? The hiring manager who accepted the recommendation? The HR leader who selected the tool?
- **How do you performance-manage a hybrid team?** If a human employee works alongside an AI agent, standard productivity metrics become meaningless. The human's value lies in judgment, creativity, and relationship skills that aren't captured by throughput measures.
- **What happens to team culture when half the "team" is software?** The informal knowledge transfer that happens through water-cooler conversations, mentorship lunches, and hallway interactions doesn't have an AI equivalent.

Companies building digital identities for AI agents are essentially creating a new employment category that sits outside every existing legal and organizational framework. The employment law implications alone will keep attorneys busy for the next decade.

## The Regulatory Landscape Is Moving Fast

The legal environment around AI in HR is shifting faster than most companies realize.

**New York City's Local Law 144** requires employers using automated employment decision tools to conduct annual bias audits and notify candidates when AI is being used. [The law has been in effect since July 2023](https://www.nyc.gov/site/dca/about/automated-employment-decision-tools.page) and enforcement actions are beginning to materialize.

**Illinois's AI Video Interview Act** requires employers to notify candidates when AI analyzes video interviews and obtain consent before using AI to evaluate applicants.

**Colorado's AI Act** (effective 2026) requires developers and deployers of "high-risk" AI systems — which explicitly includes employment decisions — to exercise reasonable care to avoid algorithmic discrimination. It includes notification requirements and provides a right of action for individuals harmed by AI-driven decisions.

**The EU AI Act** classifies AI systems used in employment, workers management, and access to self-employment as "high-risk," subjecting them to the most stringent requirements including mandatory risk assessments, documentation, transparency, and human oversight.

For multinational companies, the compliance burden is substantial and growing. An AI screening tool that's legal in Texas might violate regulations in New York, Colorado, or Brussels. Each jurisdiction has different audit, notification, and transparency requirements.

## A Practical Playbook for the Transition

If you're an HR leader navigating this transition, or a CEO wondering whether your HR function is evolving fast enough, here's what the evidence suggests works:

### Step 1: Audit Your HR Function by Task, Not by Role

Don't start by asking "which roles can we eliminate?" Start by mapping every task your HR team performs and categorizing them:

- **Automatable now:** Policy lookups, benefits enrollment, scheduling, basic reporting, resume screening for minimum qualifications
- **Augmentable:** Performance review drafting (AI drafts, human reviews), comp analysis (AI benchmarks, human approves), training recommendations (AI suggests, human contextualizes)
- **Human-essential:** Harassment investigations, termination conversations, executive coaching, organizational design, change management, bias auditing of AI tools

This task-level mapping reveals that most HR roles are bundles of automatable and human-essential work. The goal isn't to eliminate roles wholesale but to redesign them around the human-essential work.

### Step 2: Deploy AI for Service Delivery First

Start where the risk is lowest and the ROI is highest. An AI agent that answers benefits questions won't produce bias lawsuits. It will free your HR team to do higher-value work immediately. IBM spent years on this step before expanding to more complex use cases, and the sequencing matters.

```
AI HR Implementation Sequence (Risk-Adjusted):
Phase 1 (Months 1-6): Service desk automation, FAQ chatbot, benefits enrollment
Phase 2 (Months 6-12): Comp benchmarking, pay equity monitoring, basic reporting
Phase 3 (Months 12-18): Performance review augmentation, L&D personalization
Phase 4 (Months 18-24): Recruiting AI (with mandatory bias auditing)
Phase 5 (Months 24+): Predictive workforce planning, org design recommendations
```

### Step 3: Build Your Bias Auditing Capability Before You Need It

Don't wait for a lawsuit to discover your AI screening tool has a disparate impact problem. Build — or hire — the capability to audit AI outputs for bias before deploying any tool that touches hiring, promotion, or compensation decisions.

This means:

- **Demographic impact analysis** on every AI-driven decision at regular intervals
- **A/B testing** where a subset of candidates goes through the traditional process alongside the AI process
- **External audit relationships** with firms that specialize in algorithmic fairness
- **Documentation** of every model, its training data, its decision logic, and its observed outcomes

The companies that will survive the coming wave of AI hiring lawsuits aren't the ones with the best tools. They're the ones with the best audit trails.

### Step 4: Redesign Entry-Level Roles, Don't Kill Them

Take the Korn Ferry warning seriously. For every entry-level position you're tempted to replace with AI, ask: where will this team's next manager come from?

The redesigned entry-level role looks different from the old one. Instead of data entry and report compilation, the new analyst evaluates AI outputs, flags anomalies, and learns the business through the lens of the AI tools they'll eventually manage. It's a more interesting job, it pays better, and it builds the pipeline you'll need in five years.

### Step 5: Invest in the Roles AI Can't Do

The transition creates genuine opportunity to build HR capabilities that most companies have historically underinvested in:

- **Org design** that's data-driven and responsive to real-time workforce intelligence
- **Change management** that helps employees adapt to working alongside AI
- **Culture architecture** that maintains human connection in increasingly automated environments
- **Strategic workforce planning** that models multiple scenarios and prepares for each

These aren't administrative roles. They're strategic functions that justify senior compensation and carry real organizational influence. The CHRO who can navigate this transition becomes the most valuable C-suite partner the CEO has.

## The Bottom Line

HR is not going away. But the HR department that most companies operate today — large teams of generalists processing transactions, answering questions, and maintaining spreadsheets — is disappearing. IBM showed us the endgame. The Workday lawsuit showed us the risks. Korn Ferry showed us the second-order effects nobody planned for.

The companies that navigate this well will emerge with smaller, more senior, more strategic HR teams that deliver better outcomes for both the business and its employees. The companies that navigate it poorly will cut costs in the short term and face bias lawsuits, leadership pipeline crises, and cultural erosion in the medium term.

The window for thoughtful transition is closing. If you haven't started mapping your HR function against AI capabilities, you're already behind. And if you've started but skipped the bias auditing, you're building on sand.
