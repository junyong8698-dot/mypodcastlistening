## The Prediction That Got Everyone's Attention

In October 2024, [Gartner dropped a prediction](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-10-22-gartner-unveils-top-predictions-for-it-organizations-and-users-in-2025-and-beyond) that traveled through every Slack channel and LinkedIn feed in corporate America: "Through 2026, 20% of organizations will use AI to flatten their organizational structure, eliminating more than half of current middle management positions."

We're now in March 2026. So where do we actually stand?

The short answer: the prediction wasn't wrong, but the execution has been far messier than the clean narrative suggests. Some companies have aggressively cut middle management and seen real gains. Others did the same and are now quietly rehiring. The pattern is more interesting — and more instructive — than the headline.

This post breaks down what's actually happening on the ground, which roles are genuinely at risk, what the early movers got right and wrong, and what a realistic AI-augmented org structure looks like for companies that want to move fast without breaking their culture.

## What Middle Managers Actually Do (And Which Parts AI Can Replace)

Before we talk about replacing middle management, we need to be honest about what they do. The caricature — the pointy-haired boss from Dilbert who adds no value — obscures a more complex reality.

Middle managers typically handle five categories of work:

**1. Information routing and status reporting**
Collecting updates from teams, synthesizing them into dashboards or reports, and passing information up the chain. This is the most automatable function. AI agents already do this better than humans — they don't forget, they don't editorialize, and they work 24/7.

**2. Task allocation and scheduling**
Deciding who works on what, managing calendars, resolving resource conflicts. Project management tools with AI capabilities (Asana's AI features, Monday.com's automations, Linear's auto-assignment) have been chipping away at this for years. Agentic AI is finishing the job.

**3. Performance monitoring**
Tracking KPIs, identifying underperformers, flagging risks. This is where AI genuinely excels — pattern recognition across large datasets, anomaly detection, and trend analysis. [McKinsey's 2025 State of AI survey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) found that redesigning workflows around AI had the single biggest impact on organizational performance across all 25 attributes they tested.

**4. Decision-making within defined parameters**
Approving budgets under a threshold, deciding on routine escalations, choosing between predefined options. Rule-based decisions are exactly what AI handles well, especially when the decision criteria are documented.

**5. People development, coaching, and culture**
Mentoring, resolving interpersonal conflicts, building team cohesion, navigating ambiguity, advocating for reports. This is where AI falls flat — and where the companies that over-automated are now paying the price.

The companies getting this right are automating categories 1-4 and reinvesting in category 5. The ones getting it wrong are treating all five categories as equivalent.

## The Klarna Experiment: A Cautionary Tale in Three Acts

No company has been more publicly aggressive about AI-driven workforce reduction than Klarna, the Swedish buy-now-pay-later company. Their story is worth examining in detail because it contains every lesson about AI organizational transformation compressed into 18 months.

**Act 1: The Bold Move (2024)**

Klarna CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski announced that the company's AI assistant, powered by OpenAI, was [doing the work of 700 full-time customer service agents](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/14/klarna-ceo-says-ai-helped-company-shrink-workforce-by-40percent.html). The company instituted a hiring freeze and let attrition do its work. By mid-2025, Klarna had shrunk from roughly 5,527 employees to around 2,907 — a 40% reduction. The AI was handling two-thirds of all customer service conversations.

The financial results looked spectacular. Revenue per employee skyrocketed. Marketing spend dropped. Siemiatkowski told the press that AI was handling the work of [853 full-time staff](https://www.supplychainbrain.com/articles/42877-klarna-credits-ai-for-higher-salaries-as-staffing-dwindles) and the company was raising remaining employee salaries by 60%.

**Act 2: The Cracks Appear (Late 2025)**

Then the metrics that weren't in the press releases started surfacing. Customer complaints increased. Satisfaction ratings dropped. The AI handled volume well but struggled with nuanced, complex, or emotionally charged interactions — exactly the ones that determine whether a customer stays or leaves. [Reports emerged](https://mlq.ai/news/klarna-ceo-admits-aggressive-ai-job-cuts-went-too-far-starts-hiring-again-after-us-ipo/) of generic, repetitive, and insufficiently nuanced automated responses.

The problem wasn't the AI itself. The problem was that Klarna had removed the human judgment layer that knew when to escalate, how to read between the lines of a customer complaint, and when a policy exception would save a relationship worth more than the cost of the exception.

**Act 3: The Reversal (2026)**

Siemiatkowski eventually acknowledged the cuts had gone too far. Klarna started hiring again, specifically for roles that required human judgment and relationship management. A [2025 Orgvue survey](https://tandemcoach.co/klarna-ai-automation-lesson/) found that 55% of companies that made employees redundant due to AI now regret those decisions.

The lesson isn't that AI doesn't work for organizational restructuring. It's that treating AI deployment as a headcount reduction exercise instead of a role redesign exercise produces predictable failures.

## Who's Getting Organizational AI Right

Not every company followed the Klarna playbook. Some have been more thoughtful, and their results are more sustainable.

### Salesforce: The "Digital Workforce" Model

Salesforce's approach with [Agentforce](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work) is instructive. Rather than replacing roles, they added a layer of AI agents that handle specific workflow segments — simulating product launches, orchestrating marketing campaigns, handling first-line customer queries. CEO Marc Benioff described it as creating a "digital workforce" where humans and agents work together.

The key distinction: Salesforce didn't eliminate the product launch manager. They gave the product launch manager an AI team that handles the data collection, scheduling, stakeholder tracking, and status reporting so the human can focus on strategy, relationships, and the judgment calls that determine whether the launch succeeds.

### What "AI High Performers" Do Differently

McKinsey's data is revealing here. Their [2025 survey of nearly 2,000 organizations](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) found that high-performing companies consistently differ from the rest across six dimensions: strategy, talent, operating model, technology, data, and adoption.

The critical finding: **32% of companies expect AI to reduce their total workforce by at least 3% within the next year**, but AI high performers are more likely to predict *both* reductions and increases — they're restructuring, not just cutting. They're eliminating roles that are primarily information-routing while creating new roles focused on AI oversight, prompt engineering, and cross-functional coordination.

The companies that just cut headcount and declare victory are the ones that end up in the Orgvue regret statistics.

## The New Org Chart: What Flatter Actually Looks Like

When Gartner talks about "flattening" organizational structure, what does that mean in practice? Based on early adopters, the pattern looks like this:

### Before: The Traditional Pyramid

```
CEO
  └─ VP (Strategy, Budget)
       └─ Director (Department Goals, Resource Allocation)
            └─ Senior Manager (Team Oversight, Reporting)
                 └─ Manager (Task Assignment, Performance Tracking)
                      └─ Team Lead (Daily Coordination)
                           └─ Individual Contributors
```

Six layers between the person doing the work and the person setting the strategy.

### After: The AI-Augmented Diamond

```
CEO + AI Strategy Dashboard
  └─ VP (Strategy + AI-Surfaced Insights)
       └─ Team Lead / Player-Coach (People + AI Agent Oversight)
            └─ Individual Contributors + AI Agents
```

Three to four layers. The Director, Senior Manager, and Manager layers collapse because:

- **Information routing** → handled by AI dashboards and automated reports
- **Task allocation** → handled by AI project management agents
- **Performance monitoring** → handled by AI analytics with exception-based human review
- **Routine decisions** → handled by AI within defined policy parameters
- **People development** → stays human, elevated in importance

The remaining human manager role transforms into what some organizations call a "player-coach" — someone who both contributes individual work and handles the irreducibly human aspects of team leadership.

## Duolingo: The AI-First Rewrite

Duolingo provides another data point. In January 2024, the language-learning company [cut 10% of its contractor workforce](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2024/01/10/duolingo-ai-layoffs/), primarily content writers and translators, replacing them with AI. By mid-2025, CEO Luis von Ahn announced the company would [phase out contractors entirely](https://fortune.com/article/duolingo-ceo-says-getting-rid-of-contract-employees-replacing-them-with-ai/) for work AI could handle, declaring Duolingo an "AI-first" company.

The organizational impact went beyond headcount. Duolingo's content creation pipeline — which previously required human translators coordinated by project managers coordinated by content directors — collapsed into a system where a smaller team of AI trainers and quality reviewers worked directly with LLM-based generation tools.

The middle management layer that existed to coordinate human translators across 40+ languages became unnecessary when the coordination target shifted from humans to AI models.

This is the pattern that Gartner is tracking: it's not that AI replaces the manager directly. It's that AI replaces the *work that required the manager to exist*.

## The Skills That Survive Organizational Flattening

If you're a middle manager reading this and feeling uneasy, the productive question isn't "will my job be eliminated?" It's "which parts of my current role will still need a human in 18 months?"

Based on where early adopters are landing, these skills are appreciating in value:

### 1. Ambiguity Navigation

AI is excellent at optimizing within defined parameters. It's terrible at deciding which parameters matter. When a project faces competing priorities with no clear framework for resolution — a client wants faster delivery but the team is burning out, or a cost cut will save money this quarter but damage a strategic relationship — that's a human judgment call that requires understanding context, relationships, politics, and values simultaneously.

### 2. Cross-Functional Translation

As organizations flatten, the remaining human managers need to translate between domains more than ever. The engineering team, the sales team, and the finance team all have their own languages, priorities, and definitions of success. AI can surface data across these domains, but interpreting what that data means in context — and negotiating trade-offs that all parties can live with — requires human social intelligence.

### 3. Trust Building and Conflict Resolution

You cannot automate trust. When a team member is struggling, when two colleagues have a conflict that's affecting output, when someone needs to hear hard feedback delivered with empathy — these moments define whether a team functions or fractures. Companies that eliminated these roles are learning that the cost of un-managed teams shows up in attrition, and attrition is expensive.

### 4. AI Oversight and Prompt Architecture

This is the genuinely new skill. Someone needs to design the prompts and workflows that AI agents follow, monitor their outputs for quality and bias, and know when to override automated decisions. This isn't a junior role — it requires deep domain knowledge plus AI literacy. The best candidates are often experienced middle managers who understand the business context deeply enough to know when the AI is wrong.

### 5. Strategic Narrative and Change Communication

When organizations restructure — especially AI-driven restructuring that triggers job security anxiety — someone needs to explain why, paint a credible picture of the future, and maintain morale through the transition. This is leadership work that AI cannot do, and it's the work that separates successful transformations from the 55% that end in regret.

## A Practical Framework: The 70/20/10 Restructuring Model

For organizations considering AI-driven org flattening, here's a framework based on what the successful early adopters are doing:

### 70% Augmentation

Take 70% of middle management tasks and augment them with AI. Don't eliminate the role — transform it. The manager who spent 4 hours per day on status reports and task tracking now spends 30 minutes reviewing AI-generated reports and 3.5 hours on coaching, strategy, and cross-functional work.

### 20% Automation

Identify the 20% of roles that are genuinely and primarily information-routing. These are the roles where the person's main contribution is collecting data from one group and presenting it to another. Automate these, but do it with a transition plan — retraining, redeployment, or generous severance.

### 10% Creation

Create new roles that didn't exist before: AI operations managers, prompt engineers, human-AI workflow designers, AI ethics and quality reviewers. Hire from your existing middle management pool when possible — they have the domain knowledge that makes AI oversight effective.

```
Audit Prompt for Your Organization:

List every middle management role. For each one, categorize the 
time split across these five functions:

1. Information routing/reporting: ___% 
2. Task allocation/scheduling: ___%
3. Performance monitoring: ___%
4. Routine decision-making: ___%
5. People development/coaching/culture: ___%

Roles where #1-4 total > 80%: candidates for automation
Roles where #5 > 30%: candidates for augmentation and elevation
Roles where #1-4 total > 80% AND #5 < 10%: candidates for elimination

Run this audit before making any headcount decisions.
```

## The Gartner Prediction, Revisited

So is Gartner's prediction coming true? In March 2026, the answer is "partially, and not in the way most people expected."

Yes, organizations are flattening. The [McKinsey data confirms](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) that workflow redesign is the single most impactful organizational change companies can make around AI. The [IMD forecasts for 2026](https://www.imd.org/ibyimd/artificial-intelligence/2026-ai-trends-what-leaders-need-to-know-to-stay-competitive/) describe this year as the shift from scaling AI pilots to full organizational overhaul.

But the 20% of organizations doing this successfully aren't eliminating middle management wholesale. They're decomposing the role into its component functions, automating the mechanical parts, and elevating the human parts. The organizations that treated this as a cost-cutting exercise are the ones now dealing with regret, rehiring, and the morale damage that comes from employees watching their colleagues get replaced by chatbots.

The real prediction should have been: "Through 2026, 20% of organizations will fundamentally redesign middle management roles around AI capabilities, and the ones that confuse redesign with elimination will learn an expensive lesson about what humans actually do."

## What to Do This Quarter

Whether you're a CEO planning a restructure or a middle manager planning your career, here are concrete next steps:

**If you lead an organization:**
- Run the audit prompt above before making any structural changes
- Start with augmentation pilots, not elimination programs
- Measure what happens to team satisfaction and retention when you automate management tasks — not just what happens to cost
- Talk to Klarna's competitors about what happened to their customer satisfaction during the aggressive-cut phase

**If you're a middle manager:**
- Audit your own time. Honestly categorize where your hours go across the five functions above
- If more than 70% of your time is information routing and task tracking, you're in the automation zone — start shifting toward coaching, strategy, and cross-functional work now
- Learn AI tools deeply enough to oversee them, not just use them
- Position yourself as the person who knows the domain well enough to tell the AI when it's wrong

**If you're building AI agent systems:**
- Design for augmentation first, replacement second
- Build in human escalation paths that are easy to use, not friction-heavy "are you sure?" dialogues
- Instrument your systems to measure quality degradation, not just volume metrics
- Remember that the Klarna lesson applies to your customers too
